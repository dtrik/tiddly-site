Hyperfocus

Switching off Autopilot Mode

Most things in life are not a result of deliberate choice. They happen as a confluence of external factors, internalized habits and behaviors etc. It is good to have an autopilot mode for a lot of things. But attention is too important to be allocated on autopilot mode. We need to choose what we focus on at any moment deliberately. It helps us lead focused, productive and creative lives.

Today's life is overstimulating, we are bombarded with things that demand a share of our attention. Being able to focus on a single activity for a significant time has become difficult. Learning to hyperfocus is even more important.

Directing our attention towards the most important task of our choosing and then sustaining that attention is the most consequential decision we will make throughout the day. We are what we pay attention to.

There are four types of tasks:

  1. necessary work - important but not attractive. Reports, meetings etc
  2. unnecessary work - not important and not attractive. Renaming folders, wasteful work done for procrastinating
  3. distracting work - not important but attractive. Browsing, doomscrolling social media. Useless things that are blackholes of our attention.
  4. purposeful work - important and attractive. Rehearsing for an actor, develop and plan experiments for a researcher etc.

Ideally we do only 1 and 4. But usually we procrastinate on 1 and barely get time for 4, wasting time on 2 and 3. This happens due to living life on autopilot. The author found himself being able to dedicate more time to 1 and 4 and explains how in the rest of the book.
